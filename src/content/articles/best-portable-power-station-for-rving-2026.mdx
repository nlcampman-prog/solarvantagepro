## RV Watt-Hour Needs: How Much Capacity Do You Actually Need?

Selecting the perfect portable power station starts with calculating your daily power consumption. Your required storage capacity (measured in watt-hours, or Wh) depends heavily on your RV type and off-grid lifestyle:

*   **Weekend Warrior / Compact Van Lifer (300Wh – 1,000Wh):** Ideal for charging laptops, powering smartphones, running LED light strips, and operating a small portable 12V cooler or single CPAP machine.
*   **Moderate Boondocker / Travel Trailer (1,000Wh – 2,500Wh):** High enough capacity to run low-draw electronics, power your water pump, operate a microwave or blender for short periods, and power a Starlink dish for multiple hours.
*   **Full-Time Off-Grid Nomad / Large RV (2,500Wh – 5,000Wh+):** Essential if you intend to run heavy 120V appliances like a portable induction cooktop, electric space heaters, or run your RV's rooftop air conditioner during hot afternoons.

## Integrating Your Power Station with Your RV's System

Modern RVers use portable power stations in two primary ways to power their rigs:

### 1. The "Shore Power" Plug-and-Play Method
The easiest integration method. Simply use a standard 15A/30A RV dogbone adapter and plug your travel trailer’s main shore power cord directly into your power station (like the Jackery 2000 v2's native 30A plug). This instantly powers all your internal outlets, microwave, and lights. 
*   *Note:* Ensure you turn off your RV’s built-in converter/battery charger inside your fuse box, as the generator trying to charge the RV battery while the RV battery tries to run the generator will create a wasteful loop!

### 2. The Alternator Charging Method (Driving)
To replenish your power station while traveling, connect the generator's DC input port to your vehicle's 12V accessory socket. Modern units support high-draw DC charging, allowing you to replenish 100W to 200W of battery capacity simply by keeping the vehicle's engine running on the road.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### Can a portable power station run a 13,500 BTU RV air conditioner?
Yes, but only for limited durations. A standard 13,500 BTU air conditioner draws roughly 1,200W to 1,400W of continuous power. A 2,000Wh station (like the **Jackery 2000 v2** or **Anker SOLIX S2000**) can run it for approximately 1.2 to 1.5 hours. To run an AC through hot afternoons, you must combine your power station with a steady 600W+ solar panel array or add expansion battery packs.

### Can I charge my portable power station from my RV's generator or shore power?
Yes. All major power stations feature high-speed AC inputs. When you are plugged into grid shore power or running a standard onboard gas generator, you can connect the power station to an AC wall outlet to fully recharge the battery in under 1.5 to 2 hours.
